2 	423 	In principle, reannotating datasets with more complex
information is valuable . The paper frames its contribution
around the training and evaluation of VLMs, but it
overlooks an important benefit of such reannotations:
enabling researchers to analyze whether certain models
systematically fail in specific contextual scenarios and
lack of diverse scenes in their datasets. This capability
would be a much larger contribution, especially considering
that current VLMs are not yet sufficient for autonomous
driving and still require support from classical
object-detection systems.
Training VLMs such as the Qwen2-VL-2B is fine, but the
paper would be significantly stronger if the model were
used to automatically annotate additional frames with
accuracy surpassing existing tools such as ChatGPT instead
of training it for training’s sake.
Overall, the impression is that the annotation has been
done for the annotation’s sake, but in an applied field
such as autonomous driving, the practical and highly useful
implications should not be missed. Otherwise, the paper is
incomplete.

To the authors, the suggestion is therefore to expand the
paper and showcase how and why other researchers should be
using their annotations, baseline model etc.

Additionally, the formatting needs some work and some
figures are not readable. 	 	 
3 	425 	This paper introduces a frame-level dataset, called
CARScenes, designed to support interpretable, scene-level
understanding for autonomous-driving VLMs. The overall idea
is sound, but the paper shows several concerns related to
writing quality, methodological rigor, and the discussion
of results. In its current form, it reads more like a
technical report than a scientific paper.

1) the keyword list repeats the term “datasets.”

2) I evaluated the demo tool
(https://chatgpt.com/g/g-Rpba6Wp9Q-drive-scene-analyzer)
using an image from the GOOSE dataset
(2023-05-24_neubiberg_cloudy__0155_1684934442561783912_wind
shield_vis.png). The model produced several hallucinations,
including misclassifying moving vehicles as parked, merging
two lanes into one, labeling a runner as a cyclist, and
detecting street parking where none exists. It also
misidentified vehicle positions (e.g., right and ahead).
Similar issues appeared in BDD100k samples
(cabc9045-5a50690f, cadfb2fe-f43878a3, cae842b9-82d52302).

The paper should clearly acknowledge these limitations when
linking to the demo.


3) the paper does not follow standard scientific writing
conventions. Excessive bold and italic formatting, frequent
enumerations (e.g., i, ii), and irregular paragraph starts
(e.g., Sections III-B, III-C, III-E, IV-A) interrupt the
reading flow and make the paper difficult to read.


4)  figure 1 does not include weather, Vehicle.TotalNumber,
or TrafficLights.States.

5) the paper references analyses that are not clearly
reported:

- Section IV-A claims to present "prevalence of list-valued
attributes (e.g., Vehicles.Types,
Visibility.SpecificImpairments) via micro-averaged support
counts,” but these results do not appear in the text.

- Section IV-B states that "We also report summary
statistics (median, interquartile range) to characterize
the concentration of difficulty and to aid stratified
sampling." are reported, but these statistics are not
shown.


6) section V-A contains detailed training descriptions that
are uncommon in the main text of a scientific paper. Such
material is better placed in an appendix or provided in the
code repository.

7) section V reads more like a technical report rather than
a result and discussion section of a scientific paper. It
lacks analytical depth and does not interpret the findings
in a broader scientific context.

8) section V-C is descriptive and does not offer a critical
discussion of the results.

9) statements such as “These artifacts are committed
alongside the code,” repeated throughout the manuscript,
are uncommon in scientific writing and further contribute
to a technical-report style. 

10) what is the impact of the lack of temporal context in
the scene evaluation?
 	 	The attached video is edited in a way that makes the

4 	6683 	Your work addresses a meaningful gap in existing autonomous
driving dataset. Despite providing the Drive Scene
Analyzer, the dataset and the accompanying code are not
provided. It also fails to clearly describe how the
pipeline, the knowledge base, and the severity definition
were derived. The VLM-based pipeline considers
human-in-the-loop correction, but seems to be error-prone.

Remarks:
- The dataset and code are missing.
- How were the frames from the datasets selected? The
distribution of scenes and time of day (Fig. 1) is clearly
unbalanced. Could this be mitigated by a different sampling
strategy?
- How were the categories of the knowledge base and the
severity definition derived? The choices seem reasonable
but are not supported by any literature.
- Are the categories of the knowledge base sufficient to
train VLMs for high-level scene understanding?
- Some sections seem very short (e.g., III-B, III-E).
Consider reworking the structure.
- I prompted some frames with the provided Drive Scene
Analyzer and observed errors in the labels (e.g., vehicles
detected although no vehicles were present in the scene).
Human checks seem to be necessary for each frame.
- Are there any related pipelines to provide such labels?
For example, a quick search lead me to this work:  Esteban
Rivera, Jannik Lübberstedt, Nico Uhlemann, Markus Lienkamp,
"Scenario Understanding of Traffic Scenes Through Large
Visual Language Models", Proceedings of the Winter
Conference on Applications of Computer Vision (WACV)
Workshops, 2025, pp. 1037-1045 
- IV-B: Where are the summary statistics reported?
- IV-B: Daylight dominance is not limited to some sources.
- V-A: You start with (a), but no other elements follow.
- V-A (a): Are these launch commands really relevant to
include in the paper? They should be moved to the dataset
documentation.
- Table IV: Is the validation split truly human-provided
ground truth? Is each frame in the validation dataset
checked by a human?
